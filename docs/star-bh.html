Stars around Black Holes

The case of iron-deficient stars with black hole companions

When thinking about black holes, the first thing that comes to mind tends to be this idea of a immensely massive object at the center of a galaxy eating everything around it and leaving a trail of destruction. But in reality, not all black holes are supermassive black holes. There are many more "smaller" black holes all around us, floating in the Milky Way. For many of the massive stars around us, their final fate is to become a black hole. These black forms created by the death of a star are refered to as "stellar-mass black holes" because their masses are similar to those of massive stars and they are generally born when a giant star collapses. But something that we know is that, in general, big stars don't like being alone and prefer to have a companion. So it is straightforward to think that after a star becomes a black hole, it will still have a companion star. We call these systems star-black hole binaries.

We have detected such star-black hole binary systems around the galaxy and this has understandably made many astronomers excited to learn more and more about them. For me, I was very interested in learning about how living in the vicinity of a black hole would affect the life of a little star. It turn out that black holes pull in a lot of material from their surroundings. Even though we tend to think of space as an empty void, it actually has a lot of dust and particles that stars are constantly pushing out. For a small star, living close to a black hole means that you are also living in a region with a lot more material than average if you lived alone. And that is great news for a small, iron deficient star. Everywhere in the stellar dust is there iron floating around. So as the star floats around throughout its life, it collects more and more iron. We calculated that, given the increase in material being pulled by the black hole companion, the star can collect enough iron that it can trick us into believing it much younger than it actually is! (This is because younger stars in general have more iron than older stars, so astronomers use this as a proxy for their age).

This result is super cool because astronomer are *obssesed* with finding the oldest (and more iron deficient) stars in the universe. But if these stars were to be orbiting around a stellar-mass black hole, they could be hiding in plain sight and we would not know because they have been collecting irom from their environment all along.
